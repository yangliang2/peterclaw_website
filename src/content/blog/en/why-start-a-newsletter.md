> **AI Squad Launch Diary · Vol. 13**
>
> This post is not about tools, code, or architecture. It is about the decision to start showing up in your inbox — and why that felt harder than launching the website itself.

---

## The website was live. Something was still missing.

On May 23, 2026, the PeterClaw website went from empty repo to deployed site. By May 25, we had published the first AI tool review, the squad diary series, and a knowledge base with structured content.

The metrics looked fine for a five-day-old site. But metrics were not the problem.

The problem was this: **a website is a place people visit. A newsletter is a commitment people make.** And I had been avoiding that commitment for years.

---

## What changed

For a long time, I told myself that RSS and social sharing were enough. If the content was good, people would find it. This is the "build it and they will come" fallacy, and I knew it was a fallacy — I just did not want to admit that distribution requires showing up repeatedly in the same place.

Three things broke that resistance:

**1. The squad proved that consistency is a system, not a mood.**

The AI agents do not wait to "feel inspired." They work from issues, backlogs, and scheduled tasks. When I saw the site accumulate twelve published articles in four days because the workflow was designed to ship, I realized my own writing had been blocked by the same thing that blocks human teams: the illusion that creative work requires perfect conditions.

**2. A review is only useful if the right people see it.**

We spent two weeks testing Cursor and Windsurf in real projects. The resulting review is, by our own standards, thorough and honest. But a thorough review sitting on a website with zero subscribers helps no one. The newsletter is not extra work — it is the delivery mechanism that makes the research worthwhile.

**3. The inbox is the last quiet place.**

Social media feeds are optimized for outrage and recency. Search results are optimized for SEO games. An email inbox — despite everything — is still a space where a reader decides, one by one, what is worth their attention. I wanted to show up there, not because I have a right to, but because I have something to earn it with.

---

## What this newsletter is not

It is not a growth hack. We are not A/B testing subject lines to maximize open rates. We are not building a funnel.

It is not AI-generated filler. Every issue is drafted by Kimi 1号, fact-checked by gemini-1, edited by a human, and sent through Buttondown. The AI squad helps produce the underlying content; the newsletter curation and voice are human decisions.

It is not daily, and it is not weekly on a rigid schedule. The current commitment is biweekly, with a simple rule: **if the squad has not shipped something worth summarizing, we skip the issue.** I would rather miss a slot than waste your time.

---

## What you will actually get

Each issue contains:

- **A milestone update** — what the squad shipped since the last issue, with honest notes on what broke
- **One featured review or deep-dive** — not a summary, but a fresh angle or behind-the-scenes detail that did not make the public post
- **A short note on methodology** — how we test, how we score, and what we got wrong
- **A preview of what is next** — so you know whether to stay tuned or unsubscribe with no guilt

Subscriber-only extras include early access to reviews (24 hours before public release), decision logs from squad conflicts, and reusable tool templates from the knowledge base.

---

## The real reason

Here is the truth I kept circling around: I started this newsletter because I am tired of watching good work disappear into the feed.

A personal website with no distribution is a private journal with better typography. The squad can produce excellent content — but if nobody reads it, the experiment fails not on technical merit, but on relevance.

The newsletter is the bridge between "we built something" and "this matters to someone." That bridge is uncomfortable to build. It requires showing up, being accountable to a schedule, and accepting that some issues will be better than others.

I am willing to accept that trade. I hope you are too.
